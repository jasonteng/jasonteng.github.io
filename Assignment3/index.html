Ecologists discover when and how tropical trees regenerate

Nouabale-Ndoki National Park is a tree-dotted enclave in the Republic of Congo. Heavy logging surrounds the park, but it still has one of the largest intact forests in Africa. In recognition, it recently became a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Thousands of trees make up a forest. How did the trees in Nouabale-Ndoki become so numerous, and how do they stay that way?

The answer, say biologists, lies far below the tree canopy, in the soil where seedlings sprout.

The project is spearheaded by the Fred. S. Burroughs Chapter of Trout Unlimited, which is planning for a spring community ceremony celebrating the work.

In addition to replanting the ravine's walls, the goal was to re-create the stream bed and natural flood plain. In April, a large earth-moving machine was brought in and reshaped the stream with pools, natural riffles and rapids, and ways for high water to dissipate its energy, rather than dig away at the river banks.
